from scipy.stats import ttest_ind, f_oneway, chi2_contingency, pearsonr

#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
#--------------Independent t_test-------------------------
#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Extract Monthly Spend for Male customers
male_spend = Cust_Data[Cust_Data['Gender'] == 'Male']['MonthlySpend']

# Extract Monthly Spend for Female customers
female_spend = Cust_Data[Cust_Data['Gender'] == 'Female']['MonthlySpend']

# Perform independent two-sample t-test (Welch’s t-test)
# equal_var=False is used when population variances are assumed to be unequal
t_stat, p_value = ttest_ind(
    male_spend, 
    female_spend, 
    equal_var=False
)

# Display t-statistic and p-value
print("Independent t-test: Monthly Spend by Gender")
print(f"T-statistic:{t_stat:.4f}")
print(f"P-value_gender:{p_value:.4f}\n")

#--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
#-----------------One-way Anova---------------------------------
#--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Create a list of MonthlySpend arrays for each Education group
# Each element in the list represents one education category
groups = [
    group['MonthlySpend'].values
    for name, group in Cust_Data.groupby('Education')
]

# Perform one-way ANOVA test
# This checks whether the mean Monthly Spend differs across Education levels
f_stat, p_value = f_oneway(*groups)

# Display F-statistic and p-value
print("One-way ANOVA: Monthly Spend by Education level")
print(f"F-statistic:{f_stat:.4f}")
print(f"P-value_Edu:{p_value:.4f}")

#------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
#--------------Chi-square test of independence---------------------------
#------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Create a contingency table between Marital Status and Number of Pets

# Perform Chi-square test of independence

chi2, p_value, dof, expected = chi2_contingency(contingency)

# Display only the p-value
print("\nChi-square Test: Marital Status vs Number of Pets")
print(f"Chi-square statistic: {chi2:.4f}")

Run Hypothesis Tests

Purpose: Validate or reject analytical assumptions using appropriate statistical tests with a defined level of confidence.

• Define Null and Alternate Hypotheses
Formulate a null hypothesis (H₀) representing no effect or no difference, and an alternate hypothesis (H₁) indicating the presence of a statistically significant effect or relationship.
• Select Appropriate Statistical Test
Choose the test based on variable types (categorical or numerical), number of groups, and the objective of comparison (e.g., t-test, ANOVA, chi-square, correlation).
• Check Statistical Assumptions
Verify key assumptions including normality, independence of observations, and homogeneity of variance to ensure the validity of the test results.
• Interpret Results
Evaluate p-values and confidence intervals to determine statistical significance and assess the strength and reliability of the findings.

Statistical hypothesis testing was conducted at a 5% significance level (95% confidence) to validate key business assumptions. Appropriate statistical tests were selected based on variable types and analytical objectives. Prior to result interpretation, critical assumptions including normality, independence of observations, and homogeneity of variance were thoroughly verified to ensure the reliability of the findings.

Understanding Statistical Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

The null hypothesis assumes that there is no effect, no difference, or no relationship between variables. It represents the default business assumption and serves as the baseline against which statistical evidence is evaluated. Statistical tests are designed to assess whether sufficient evidence exists to reject H₀.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁)

The alternate hypothesis assumes that a statistically significant effect, difference, or relationship exists between variables. It represents the analytical claim and is supported when the null hypothesis is accepted after rejecting the null hypothesis based on statistical evidence.

1. Gender-based Spending Behavior

Null Hypothesis (H₀): There is no significant difference in average monthly spend between male and female customers.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁): There is a significant difference in average monthly spend between male and female customers.

Test Used: Independent t-test

Result & Interpretation:
Since the p-value (0.7345) is greater than 0.05, the null hypothesis was not rejected. This indicates that gender does not significantly influence monthly spending.

2. Education Level and Monthly Spend

Null Hypothesis (H₀): Average monthly spend is equal across all education levels.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁): At least one education level differs in average monthly spend.

Test Used: One-way ANOVA

Result & Interpretation:
With a p-value of 0.9224 (> 0.05), no statistically significant difference was observed. Education level does not impact monthly spending.

3. Marital Status and Pet Ownership

Null Hypothesis (H₀): Marital status and number of pets owned are independent.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁): Marital status and number of pets owned are associated.

Test Used: Chi-square test of independence

Result & Interpretation:
The p-value (< 0.05) indicates a statistically significant association between marital status and pet ownership.

4. Age and Activity Level

Null Hypothesis (H₀): There is no relationship between age and days since last interaction.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁): A significant relationship exists.

Test Used: Correlation analysis

Result & Interpretation:
The correlation was weak and statistically insignificant (p = 0.6817), suggesting that customer age does not affect engagement level.

5. State-wise Spending Variation

Null Hypothesis (H₀): Mean monthly spend is the same across all states.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁): At least one state differs.

Test Used: One-way ANOVA

Result & Interpretation:
